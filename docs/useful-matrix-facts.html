Chapter 4 Useful matrix facts

4.1 Notation

A matrix \(A\) with \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns (an \(m\times n\) matrix) will be denoted as \[A = \begin{bmatrix} A_{1,1} & A_{2,1} & \cdots & A_{1,n} \\ A_{2,1} & A_{2,1} & \cdots & A_{2,n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ A_{m,1} & A_{m,2} & \cdots & A_{m,n} \\ \end{bmatrix}. \]

where, \(A_{i,j}\) denotes the element in row \(i\) and column \(j\) of matrix \(A\).

4.2 Basic mathematical properties

4.2.1 Addition and subtraction

Consider matrices \(A\) and \(B\) with identical sizes \(m\times n\).

We add \(A\) and \(B\) by adding the element in position \(i,j\) of \(B\) with the element in position \(i,j\) of \(A\), i.e.,

\[(A + B)_{i,j} = A_{i,j} + B_{i,j}.\]

Similarly, if we subtract \(B\) from matrix \(A\), then we subtract the element in position \(i,j\) of \(B\) from the element in position \(i,j\) of \(A\), i.e.,

\[(A - B)_{i,j} = A_{i,j} - B_{i,j}.\]

Example:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 9 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 11 & 4 \\ 5 & 8 & 7 \\ \end{bmatrix}.\]

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 9 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & -7 & 2 \\ 3 & 2 & 5 \\ \end{bmatrix}.\]

4.2.2 Scalar multiplication

Consider an \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\).

A matrix multiplied by a scalar value \(c\in\mathbb{R}\) is the matrix obtained by multiplying each element of the matrix by \(c\), i.e., \[(cA)_{i,j} = cA_{i,j}.\] Example: \[3\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 3\cdot 1 & 3\cdot 2 & 3\cdot 3 \\ 3\cdot 4 & 3\cdot 5 & 3\cdot 6 \\ \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 & 9 \\ 12 & 15 & 18 \\ \end{bmatrix}.\]

4.2.3 Matrix multiplication

Consider two matrices \(A\) and \(C\). The matrix product \(AC\) is only defined if the number of columns in \(A\) matches the number of columns in \(C\).

Assume \(A\) is an \(m\times n\) matrix and \(C\) is an \(n\times p\) matrix. \(AC\) will be an \(m\timesp\) matrix and \[(AC)_{i,j} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} A_{i,k}C_{k,j}.\]

Example: \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4\\ 2 & 5\\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 3 & 1 \cdot 4 + 2 \cdot 5 + 3 \cdot 6\\ 4\cdot 1 + 5 \cdot 2 + 6 \cdot 3 & 4 \cdot 4 + 5 \cdot 5 + 6 \cdot 6\\ \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 14 & 32\\ 32 & 77\\ \end{bmatrix}.\]

4.2.4 Associative property

Addition and multiplication satisfy the associative property for matrices. Assuming that the matrices \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) have the sizes required to do the operations below, then

\[(A + B) + C = A + (B + C)\] and \[(AB)C=A(BC).\]

4.2.5 Distributive property

Matrix operations satisfy the distributive property. Assuming that the matrices \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) have the sizes required to do the operations below, then

\[A(B+C)=AB + AC\quad\mathrm{and}\quad (A+B)C = AB + BC.\]

4.2.6 No commutative property

In general, matrix multiplication does not satisfy the commutative property, i.e., \[AB \neq BA,\] even when the matrix sizes allow the operations to be performed.

Example:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \end{bmatrix}\]

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2\\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\]

4.3 Transpose and related properties

4.3.1 Definition

The transpose of a matrix, denoted \(T\) as a superscript, exchanges the rows and columns of the matrix. More formally, the \(i,j\) element of \(A^T\) is the \(j,i\) element of \(A\), i.e., \((A^T)_{i,j} = A_{j,i}\).

Example:

\[\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 9 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4\\ 9 & 5\\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}.\]

4.3.2 Transpose and mathematical operations

Assume that \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) are matrices such that each of the matrix operations can be below. Additionally, assume that \(c\in \mathbb{R}\) is a scalar constant.

The following properties are true:

  • \(c^T = c\)
  • \((A + B)^T = A^T + B^T\)
  • \((AB)^T = B^T A^T\) which can be extended to \((ABC)^T=C^T B^T A^T\), etc.
  • \((A^T)^T=A\)

4.4 Special matrices

4.4.1 Square matrices

A matrix is square if the number of rows equals the number of columns. The diagonal elements of an \(n\times n\) square matrix \(A\) are the elements A_{i,i} for \(i = 1, 2, \ldots, n\). Any non-diagonal elements of \(A\) are called off-diagonal elements.

4.4.2 Identity matrix

The \(n\times n\) identity matrix \(I_{n\timesn}\) is 1 for its diagonal elements and 0 for its off-diagonal elements.

Example:

$I_{3} = \[\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

4.4.3 Symmetric

A matrix \(A\) is symmetric if \(A = A^T\). Only square matrices can be symmetric (but not all square matrices are symmetric.)

4.4.4 Idempotent

A matrix is idempotent if \(AA = A\). Only square matrices can be idempotent (but not all square matrices are idempotent).

4.5 Matrix inverse

An \(n\times n\) square matrix \(A\) is invertible if there exists a matrix \(B\) such that \(AB=BA=I_{n\times n}\). The inverse of \(A\) is denoted \(A^{-1}\).

Inverse matrices do not exist for non-square matrices.

Some other properties of the matrix inverse:

  • If \(n\times n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\) are invertible then \((AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A ^{-1}\).
  • If \(A\) is invertible then \((A^-1)^T = (A^T)^{-1}\).

4.6 Matrix derivatives

\(f(b)\) \(df(b)/db\)

The derivative of \(f(\mathbf{b})\) with respect to \(\mathbf{b}\) is denoted \(df(\mathbf{b})/d\mathbf{b}\) and \[\frac{df(\mathbf{b})}{d\mathbf{b}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{df(\mathbf{b})}{db_1}\\ \frac{df(\mathbf{b})}{db_2}\\ \vdots \\ \frac{df(\mathbf{b})}{db_p} \end{bmatrix}.\]
